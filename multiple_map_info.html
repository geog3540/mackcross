Science Education Spatial Trends in Iowa Counties

<- CLICK on image to access map! This map considers connections between educational resource investment (as measured through variables such as spending per student) and measures of student success (as measured through variables such as ACT science scores and graduation rate within 4 years. The spatial patterns can be viewed from classes ranging in size from 3-7 classes. When using the quantile classification, unfortunately, the disparity of population density between Iowa counties is masked, with less densely populated areas placed in the same class as the more rare, urban spaces such as Des Moines. While for some projects, this may not be an issue, however, given that the purpose of this work is to show how investment in education may be tied to population density, this classification fails by grouping non-urban spaces into more urban areas, as the quantile classification requires an equal number of features in each class regardless of their similarity or dissimilarity. This can be somewhat mediated by selecting more classes, however, note that even with seven classes, the final class in the population density map is 76-890, while all other classes have a much smaller range. Given this obscurity, its difficult to draw meaningful comparisons between population density and the other variables, especially considering the other variables are not nearly as diverse in their values. The equal interval classification is interesting. In terms of population density, this map performs better than quantile in terms of depicting the disparity of rural versus urban spaces. Although, the color scheme fails as, depending on the number of classes, it can be difficult to differentiate between colors since they are so sparse. In terms of the other variables, it seems that generally counties do not struggle to have students graduate, although the more eastern counties are lighter than the rest of the counties, indicating less graduates. Although, I had anticipated seeing a pattern of more urban spaces investing more in their students, this pattern was not present in the equal interval classification. There was not a clear pattern in ACT scores. However, this classification also has downsides, including, for instance, that two counties which share the same value may be in different classes--for instance, Lee County in the Percentage of Student Graduates map has a value of 88, while Wright County also has a value of 88, yet both are in different classes. This also creates a false visual, as counties of the same value should be in the same class. Lastly, with the natural breaks classification, these maps can only be evaluated individually, as this classification is not conducive to comparison as they each follow different break structures. Individually, the maps do seem to better show the underlying spatial structure of the data, as values dissimilar values are not forced together into the same class. Again, the ACT scores do not seem to have a clear spatial pattern. It is possible that only students who are planning to go to college take the ACT score and, as a result, they may not be great indicators of student success. Another element which became evident when viewing the maps was that one of the counties that spends the most on their students (Van Buren) does not have associated ACT score values (NaN), which is unfortunate in evaluating trends of investment and measures of student success.
